## Introduction

Parkinson's disease (PD) affects approximately 10 million people worldwide and is the second
most common neurodegenerative disorder after Alzheimer's disease [1]. First described by James
Parkinson in 1817 as the "shaking palsy," PD is clinically defined by cardinal motor symptoms
including resting tremor, bradykinesia, rigidity, and postural instability [2].

The pathological hallmark of PD involves the selective loss of dopaminergic neurons in the
substantia nigra pars compacta and the presence of intraneuronal protein aggregates called
Lewy bodies, which are primarily composed of misfolded alpha-synuclein protein [3]. While
the exact cause of PD remains incompletely understood, both genetic and environmental
factors contribute to its pathogenesis.

## Current Therapeutic Approaches

### Pharmacological Interventions

Levodopa, a dopamine precursor, remains the gold standard for symptomatic treatment of PD.
When combined with carbidopa (a peripheral decarboxylase inhibitor), levodopa effectively
alleviates motor symptoms in the majority of patients [4]. However, long-term use is
associated with motor complications including dyskinesias and wearing-off phenomena.

Dopamine agonists (e.g., pramipexole, ropinirole) and monoamine oxidase type B (MAO-B)
inhibitors (e.g., rasagiline, selegiline) are also used as monotherapy in early PD or as
adjuncts to levodopa therapy [5].

### Deep Brain Stimulation

Deep brain stimulation (DBS) has emerged as an effective surgical intervention for patients
with advanced PD who experience significant motor fluctuations despite optimal medical
therapy. DBS targets the subthalamic nucleus (STN) or globus pallidus internus (GPi),
providing continuous high-frequency electrical stimulation that modulates aberrant neural
activity in the basal ganglia-thalamocortical circuit [6].

Clinical trials have demonstrated that STN-DBS significantly reduces motor symptoms, decreases
levodopa requirements, and improves quality of life compared to best medical therapy alone [7].

### Emerging Neuroprotective Strategies

A major unmet need in PD therapy is the development of disease-modifying treatments that
can slow or halt neurodegeneration. Several neuroprotective approaches are currently under
investigation:

Alpha-synuclein-targeting therapies include immunotherapy approaches using antibodies that
target pathological forms of alpha-synuclein, as well as small molecules that inhibit its
aggregation [8].

Gene therapy approaches using adeno-associated viral (AAV) vectors to deliver therapeutic
genes to the brain have shown promise in preclinical models. Strategies include delivery of
neurotrophic factors (e.g., GDNF, neurturin) and RNA interference-based silencing of
alpha-synuclein [9].

## Biomarker Discovery

The identification of reliable biomarkers for PD is critical for early diagnosis, monitoring
disease progression, and assessing therapeutic efficacy. Promising biomarkers include:

- **Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) biomarkers**: Reduced levels of alpha-synuclein and altered
  phosphorylation patterns have been identified in PD patients [10].
- **Neuroimaging biomarkers**: Dopamine transporter (DAT) scanning using single-photon
  emission computed tomography (SPECT) can detect early nigrostriatal degeneration [11].
- **Digital biomarkers**: Wearable sensor technologies can objectively quantify motor
  symptoms and tremor in clinical and home settings [12].

## Conclusions

Significant progress has been made in understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying
Parkinson's disease and in developing therapeutic interventions. However, disease-modifying
treatments remain an unmet clinical need. Future research should focus on identifying
reliable biomarkers for early diagnosis, elucidating the complex interplay between genetic
and environmental factors, and developing targeted neuroprotective strategies.
